Tell me about the time when you failed.

- Nobody is perfect, and I have also learned important lessons from failures during my testing journey.

- One situation I clearly remember was early in my automation experience with Cypress. 
  I was assigned to automate a large feature, and I became too focused on building a highly reusable and generic framework. 
  I ended up creating a very complex implementation with many switch-case conditions instead of focusing on delivering the 
  actual test coverage quickly.

- Although the framework was reusable, I missed the sprint deadline for completing the automation tasks, 
  which impacted the team’s validation timeline.

- That experience taught me an important lesson about balancing engineering quality with delivery timelines. 
  Since then, I focus first on building a minimum viable automation solution to meet deadlines, 
  and then improve or refactor the framework incrementally when needed.

How will you handle an uncooperative colleague?

- I would first try to understand the reason behind the uncooperative behavior 
  instead of making assumptions.
- Sometimes, a colleague may be busy, under pressure, or may feel that the questions
  being asked are already documented. In such cases, I first check on my side whether 
  I can improve my preparation, ask clearer questions, or refer existing documentation 
  before reaching out.
- If the person is difficult to connect with due to workload or time constraints, 
  I prefer scheduling a focused discussion with clear agendas so that we can resolve all 
  queries efficiently in one go.
- However, if the behavior continues to impact team collaboration or project delivery 
  unnecessarily, then I would involve my lead or senior stakeholders professionally, 
  because ultimately everyone is working toward the same project goals.
